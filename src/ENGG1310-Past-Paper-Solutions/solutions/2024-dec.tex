\begin{qsolution}{2024}{Dec}{Q31}{\tgElectronics \tgElectronicsSemiconductor}

    \begin{parts}
        \part Boron has 3 valence $\mathrm e^-$, and silicon has 4.
        When doping silicon with boron, only 3 valence $\mathrm e^-$ are available for bonding, leaving one positive $\mathrm h^+$ (hole) in the crystal lattice, thus creating a p-type semiconductor.

        Besides, boron has a similar atomic radius to silicon, which allows it to fit into the silicon lattice without causing significant distortion, making it a suitable dopant for silicon.

        \part A hole is the absence of an electron.
        The hole itself does not move independently.
        When an electron gets excited from the valence band to the conduction band, it leaves behind a hole (positive charge carrier) in the valence band.
        Then, when a nearby valent electron jumps to fill the hole, it creates a new hole at its original position, thus the positive charge is shifted to the new position.
        This process repeats, allowing holes to ``move'' through the semiconductor material.

        \textit{(Sketch omitted.)}
    \end{parts}

\end{qsolution}

\begin{qsolution}{2024}{Dec}{Q32}{\tgElectronics \tgElectronicsFibreOptics}

    \begin{parts}
        \part The three main components of an optical fibre, from the innermost to the outermost, are the core, cladding, and jacket.
        The core usually has a refractive index of $1.5$, while the cladding has a slightly lower refractive index of $1.4$.

        \textit{(Sketch omitted.)}

        \part When the signal light rays enter the fibre, they will travel through different paths.
        The rays that travel straight through the core will arrive at the destination first.
        Some other rays traveling at an angle will reflect off the core-cladding interface, thus taking a longer path and arriving at the receiving end later.
        Since the light rays arrive at different times, the signal will be elongated when it reaches the destination, resulting in a distortion of the signal, which is called \textit{modal dispersion}.

        \textit{(Sketch omitted.)}
    \end{parts}

\end{qsolution}
